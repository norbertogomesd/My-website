1. Introduction

Mangrove forests are unique and highly productive coastal ecosystems found in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide. They play an essential role in maintaining biodiversity, protecting coastlines from erosion and storms, supporting fisheries, and sequestering significant amounts of carbon, thus contributing to climate change mitigation.

In Timor-Leste, the Metinaro coastline historically supported extensive mangrove forests that provided ecological, economic, and social benefits to local communities. However, these ecosystems have been degraded significantly over the years. The loss of mangroves in Metinaro has led to increased coastal vulnerability, declining fishery productivity, and reduced capacity for carbon sequestration.

Recognizing the urgent need for action, this project aims to restore mangrove ecosystems in the Metinaro coastal area through a sustainable, community-based approach. This restoration initiative seeks to combine scientific methods with traditional knowledge, emphasizing education, capacity building, and sustainable livelihoods.

2. Project Objectives

  • Restore at least 100 hectares of degraded mangrove habitat within five years.
  • Increase carbon storage in above-ground biomass and soils.
  • Engage and train at least 500 local community members in restoration and sustainable management.
  • Develop sustainable alternative livelihoods linked to mangrove conservation.
  • Establish a monitoring and evaluation framework to track progress and impact.
  • Raise awareness about the importance of mangroves for climate resilience and biodiversity.

3. Project Location

The Metinaro subdistrict, located approximately 15 kilometers east of Dili, is characterized by a tropical monsoon climate with distinct wet and dry seasons. The coastal zone includes sandy beaches and intertidal mudflats historically covered by diverse mangrove species. Degradation over recent decades has left large areas denuded or sparsely vegetated.

The project will focus on identified degraded sites along the Metinaro coastline that exhibit suitable environmental conditions for mangrove restoration, including tidal inundation patterns, soil salinity, and hydrological connectivity.

4. Restoration Methodology

Site Preparation

Before planting, comprehensive site assessments will evaluate hydrology, soil conditions, and threats such as invasive species. Debris and waste will be removed carefully to minimize further disturbance.

Species Selection and Propagation

Native species such as Rhizophora apiculata, Avicennia marina, Sonneratia alba, and Bruguiera gymnorrhiza will be propagated in nurseries and planted using direct seeding and transplantation methods.

Community Involvement

Local community members will be actively involved in nursery management, planting, monitoring, and maintenance through training and awareness programs.

Sustainable Livelihood Integration

Alternative livelihoods like sustainable aquaculture, ecotourism, and non-timber forest product development will be supported to reduce pressure on mangrove resources.

5. Implementation Schedule

Year Activities
Year 1 Baseline surveys, nursery establishment, community training, site preparation.
Year 2 Large-scale planting, continued training, initial monitoring.
Year 3 Planting expansion, livelihood support, mid-term evaluation.
Year 4 Maintenance, local management strengthening, capacity building.
Year 5 Final evaluation, management transfer, project closure.

7. Project Team

  • Project Manager: Oversees overall coordination and stakeholder engagement.
  • Ecological Specialist: Leads restoration design and ecological monitoring.
  • Community Liaison Officer: Facilitates community engagement and capacity building.
  • Nursery Manager: Manages seedling propagation and nursery operations.
  • Livelihoods Specialist: Supports development of alternative income activities.
  • Monitoring and Evaluation Officer: Tracks project progress and reports.
  • Local Community Representatives: Ensure cultural appropriateness and community involvement.

8. Monitoring and Evaluation

Ecological Monitoring

Permanent plots will be established to monitor seedling survival, growth, species diversity, and soil carbon content. Remote sensing and drone imagery will support large-scale assessments.

Social Monitoring

Community participation, awareness, and livelihood improvements will be assessed through surveys and interviews.

Financial Monitoring

Budget tracking and livelihood income generation will be monitored to ensure financial sustainability.

Evaluation Schedule

  • Baseline: Months 1-3
  • Mid-term: End of Year 3
  • Final: End of Year 5

9. Conclusion

The Greening the Metinaro Coast project represents a vital effort to restore coastal resilience, enhance biodiversity, and mitigate climate change impacts through sustainable mangrove restoration in Timor-Leste. By actively involving local communities and integrating scientific approaches, this initiative seeks to deliver lasting environmental, social, and economic benefits.

Continued monitoring, capacity building, and adaptive management will ensure the project’s success and sustainability. We look forward to fostering partnerships and collaboration that can expand the impact and replicate this model across other vulnerable coastal zones.
